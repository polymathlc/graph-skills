üéØ Key Rules:

  1. Use a ruler for straight lines

‚úÖ A good best-fit line minimizes the total distance from all points to the line!

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Must the best-fit line pass through some of the measured points? ‚ñº

No! The best-fit line does NOT need to pass through any specific points.

The line represents the overall trend of your data. It might pass through some points, all points, or no points at all - and that's perfectly fine!

In this example, the best-fit line doesn't pass through any points exactly, but it shows the trend correctly.

Should there be equal points above and below the line? ‚ñº

Yes, approximately! This is a good rule of thumb.

A good best-fit line should have roughly the same number of points above it as below it. This helps ensure the line represents the middle of your data.

‚úÖ Good - balanced

‚ùå Bad - unbalanced

Must the line pass through (0,0) if there's a point there? ‚ñº

Not necessarily! The origin (0,0) is just another data point.

Treat it like any other point when drawing your best-fit line. The line should show the overall trend, whether or not that includes the origin.

ü§î Only force your line through (0,0) if you have a scientific reason to believe the relationship must start at zero (e.g., if there's no light, there's no photosynthesis).
Where should axis labels be placed? ‚ñº

Both positions are acceptable!

‚úÖ At the END of the axis

‚úÖ Along the SIDE of the axis

Just make sure:

  • Labels are clear and readable
  • Include the unit in brackets
  • Don't write over your data points!
What if my points don't form a straight line? ‚ñº

If your data shows a curve, draw a smooth curve of best fit instead!

For curved data:

  • Draw freehand (no ruler)
  • Make the curve smooth - no jagged lines
  • Still aim for equal points above and below
